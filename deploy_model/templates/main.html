About the styles

Henri IV, Louis XIII - At the dawn of the XVIIth century, Henri IV invited foreign furniture makers to France. The country quickly started to set European design trends with heavy furniture, ample use of spiral-turned columns and inlaid marquetery.

Baroque - The Baroque style blossommed in the late XVIIth and early XVIIIth centuries under the grandiose Louis XIV and more comfortable Louis XV style. Commodes replaced chests. Armchairs and sofas replaced chairs. Lines became curved and decorations exuberant.

Neoclassicism - The first phase of Neoclassicism, the Louis XVI style, featured simpler, straighter lines and patterns borrowed from ancient Rome. Neoclassicism culminated under the Empire style, with classical but also Egyptian influence.

Art Nouveau - Art Nouveau revolutionized furniture design at the end of the XIXth century by taking inspiration from the complex shapes of nature: plants, animals, insects.

Art Deco - Between World War I and World War II, furniture makers were inspired by the cubist esthetic and exotic art from Asia and Africa. Bright colors, straight lines, bold patterns defined the new Art Deco style.

About the model

Motivation - Prove that a computers can recognize not only objects, but also styles. A Louis XVI chair is arguably more similar to any other chair than to a Louis XVI table, yet the model is able to group the chair and the table together.

Accuracy - 80% accuracy on the test set. Quite impressive since most humans would do no better than a random guess (ie 25% accuracy) and since styles can be ambiguous (early Louis XIV is very close to Louis XIII for instance).

Code - Transfer learning starting from a Resnet 50 model. More details here.
